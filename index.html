Commiting Money to a Goal



Rather than tryng to reduce the number of steps to simplify the process, I propose increasing the number of possible steps by making each steps shorter and clearer.

I ran three twenty minute unbiased usability tests to set a baseline for the project. I gave each participant three tasks:

  • Task1: Sign up and commit to clearing out your inbox at least 4 days a week.
  • Task2: Sign up and commit to walking 10,000 steps at least 5 days a week.
  • Task3: Change your inbox commitment to 5 days a week.

0 participants were able to complete all three tasks in 20 minutes. What's worse is that rather than just not being able to accomplish the tasks, one participant silently failed, believing she had connected her gmail account when she did not


I have put screen recordings of all three testing sessions into a dropbox available at the end of teh proposal, along with tester profiles and an executive summary.

The test revealed that the most salient problem with the user journey is that it forks at step 3 on the goal creation page.

Hick’s Law states that time it takes for a user to make a decision depends on the number of choices available — the higher the number the longer the decision time.

Giving users multiple options (to select a type of goal or connect a tracker service) before they understand the conventions of an interface is easily avoidable.

I have tried to minimise teh number of call to actions on each page by breaking up the sign up process into a simple progression of short steps. Further minimising potential errors by providing status indicators, an undo button and feedback about the progress on each step.

USING CURRENT THE CURRENT GRAPH

Another great universal design principle we love is Fitts’s Law, which essentially says that the time required to move and interact with a target area is a function of the distance and size of the target. The closer and larger the target, the faster the action. This is why we have increased the size of many UI elements, such as form fields, buttons, cards and links. Overall, it improves general usability (Fitts’s Law) and ease of use in touch-enabled platforms.



TWO TYPFACES

The display font is Trueno Semi Bold. I wanted to select a typeface that was almost indistinguishle from you current logo typeface. I chose Trieno because of all teh options I shortlisted, this typeface’s letterforms had teh most space inside each of teh letters. Wide apertures and the large counterforms result make a text more readable.

Because there is a lot of text block on your website I searched for a typeface that readable in large blocks of text. It needed to match the display type but it also needed to work as in all caps.


COLOURS

I've used the slightest hint of orange and green for success and danger notifiers but otehrwise I have stuck to a Monochromatic colour palette.

For most part, color is associated with specific actions: error states, primary and secondary button press responses and controls. This makes these prominent and distinguishable from other surrounding elements without relying on colour for teh site to make sense

Using color sparingly, and with a defined and limited purpose means you the site can be used just as easily with a vision impairments.
